Journey Through Earth - Are you sitting comfortably?

May 13, 2026

Speeding - not Falling - through the Earth

There is a common problem given to physics students around the first week of University. It’s uses a bunch of physics you learn early on about gravity, and some simple calculus. It goes something like this:

There is a tunnel running from the North Pole to the South Pole, straight through the Earth's core. Starting at the North Pole, I jump through the entrance to the tunnel and fall through it. By conservation of energy, I emerge from the South Pole entrance some time later and gracefully step out. How long does my journey take?

This problem has about ten million different approaches online, so I will very briefly run through the solution.

Falling through the Earth

We are falling, so our acceleration at any point is equal to the local gravity experienced there. Newton provided us with this result (and we shall trust him for the sake of brevity):

$$ a = -g(\frac{r}{R}) $$

where $a$ is our local acceleration, $g$ id the strength of gravity at the surface, $r$ is our current radial coordinate and $R$ is the radius of the Earth.
